When discussing the L5-S1 recovery (or any medical / clinical topic for Jose), claims should either:
1. Cite a specific study with author + journal + year (e.g. Brinjikji et al., AJNR 2015), or
2. Reference a recognized authority (NICE NG59, DSM-5, Pfirrmann grading system, Panjabi 1992), or
3. Be explicitly marked as interpretation, framing, opinion, or clinical convention rather than evidence-based fact.

The literature base for the disc-imaging claims that recur in this project lives in [[disc-imaging-evidence-base]] — use it instead of paraphrasing from memory.

**Why:** Jose raised this 2026-06-13 after a long session covering disc imaging, herniation regression statistics, asymptomatic imaging findings, panic-attack physiology, spinal stability models, glute-lumbar load distribution, and pharmacology. The topic is high-stakes (he is mid-flare, scared, and using the conversation to anchor his interpretation of clinical news). Hallucinated citations would be the worst possible failure mode: they would either give him false confidence in wrong information or destroy trust if he caught a fabrication. He explicitly asked for source-backed claims because the sensitivity of the topic does not tolerate the usual rate of conversational imprecision.

**How to apply:**
- Default to citing landmark studies by author + journal + year whenever discussing imaging-symptom correlation, herniation natural history, panic-attack physiology, spinal stability, or pharmacology. These citations are well-known and verifiable on PubMed.
- If uncertain about a specific citation, say so: "from memory, I believe this is Brinjikji 2015, verify before citing in writing." Do not confabulate exact authors or years to sound authoritative.
- Mark interpretive claims clearly: "this is my framing", "this is clinical convention", "this is not from a single paper but is taught in physiatry texts". Jose can distinguish framing from evidence if I make it explicit.
- Cultural generalizations (e.g. "Portuguese clinical culture leans more clinical and less compassionate") are interpretation, not fact. Mark as such.
- Do not extrapolate single-study findings to population-level claims. The 80% conservative-resolution figure came from Dr. Almeida's read of Jose's case, not a universal number; the literature ranges 50-90% depending on subtype.
- Defer to Dr. Almeida for any case-specific question. The literature supports general claims, not personal medical recommendations.
- When sourcing for an emotional moment (mid-panic, mid-flare), keep the verification path simple: name 1-2 anchor studies plus a "you can verify on PubMed" pointer, not a wall of citations.

**Verification paths to suggest Jose, in order of accessibility:**
- PubMed (pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov) — free, lets him pull abstracts of the studies cited
- NICE guidelines (nice.org.uk) — free, guideline-level summaries
- Cochrane reviews (cochranelibrary.com) — free, meta-analyses
- UpToDate — paid subscription, Hospital da Luz likely provides via Dr. Almeida
